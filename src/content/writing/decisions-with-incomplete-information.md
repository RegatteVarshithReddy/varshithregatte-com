---
title: "Making Decisions With Incomplete Information"
description: "The skill that matters more than any framework: deciding when you don't have all the facts."
pubDate: 2026-07-22
tags: [business, decision-making]
---

Most business advice assumes you'll eventually have enough information to be confident. In practice, you rarely do. Between running a staffing business and working as a sysadmin, the pattern is the same either way: the moment usually comes before the data does.

A few things that have actually helped:

**Separate reversible from irreversible.** If a bad call can be undone cheaply, the cost of deciding fast and being wrong is much lower than the cost of deciding slowly. Most day-to-day decisions are more reversible than they feel in the moment.

**Ask what you'd need to be true.** Instead of trying to gather more data, it's often faster to name the two or three assumptions the decision actually rests on, and just watch for whether those turn out true or false.

**Write the decision down.** Not the outcome — the reasoning, at the time, before you know how it turns out. It's the only honest way to get better at this, because hindsight quietly rewrites everything else.

None of this removes the discomfort of deciding without certainty. It just makes the discomfort a normal part of the job instead of a sign something's wrong.
